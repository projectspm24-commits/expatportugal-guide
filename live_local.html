Integration guide

We live in Portugal.
Let's actually belong here.

The gap between expats and locals doesn't have to grow. Language, culture, and community — here's how to genuinely connect with Portugal.

🇵🇹 🤝 🌍
Why this page exists
The gap is real.
And we can close it.
Portugal's expat population has grown enormously in the past decade. With it has come a real tension — rising rents, language barriers, cultural misunderstandings, and two communities that increasingly live parallel lives in the same streets.

This page isn't about blame. It's about recognising that if you choose to make Portugal your home, you have a responsibility — and an incredible opportunity — to actually become part of it.
The language barrier
Only 1 in 3 expats in Portugal make a serious effort to learn Portuguese. Yet locals consistently say that even basic Portuguese — "bom dia", "obrigado", "pode repetir?" — completely transforms how they see you.
The income gap
Many expats earn foreign salaries in a local economy. Being conscious of where you spend your money — choosing local businesses over international chains — makes a meaningful difference to the community around you.
The cultural opportunity
Portugal has one of the richest cultures in Europe. Fado, football, food, family — there is so much to fall in love with here, if you step outside the expat bubble and let it in.

Cultural guide
How Portugal actually works
The unwritten rules that no guidebook tells you — but that locals notice immediately.
💋
Greetings: the two-kiss rule
Women greet everyone (men and women) with two kisses on the cheek — right cheek first. Men greet women with kisses and men with a handshake, unless they're close friends. Always greet everyone individually when entering a room — walking in and giving a general wave is considered rude.
Greet each person individuallyDon't wave at a group
🕐
Time is... flexible
Being 15–20 minutes late to a social event is perfectly normal and expected in Portugal. Arriving exactly on time can actually make your host feel rushed. For business, be punctual. For dinner at someone's home, 20 minutes late is fine — and bring wine or flowers.
Bring wine or flowers to dinnerDon't arrive exactly on time to social events
🍽️
Lunch is sacred
Lunch in Portugal is the main meal of the day — often 1–2 hours, especially in smaller towns. Scheduling business meetings over lunch unless explicitly invited is a faux pas. The prato do dia (daily special) at a local tasca is the authentic lunch experience. Don't rush it.
Order the prato do diaDon't eat a sandwich at your desk
🎁
Tipping
Tipping is appreciated but not expected the way it is in the US or UK. Rounding up the bill or leaving €1–2 for a coffee is generous. For restaurants, 5–10% is plenty. Never tip with a card — always cash. Don't over-tip ostentatiously; it can come across as patronising.
Tip in cashRound up the billDon't tip 20%
🗣️
Try Portuguese — always
Even broken, halting Portuguese is received with warmth and respect. Starting every interaction with at least a greeting in Portuguese — "Bom dia, fala inglês?" — signals respect. Jumping straight into English signals entitlement. Portuguese people will often switch to English to help you, but they remember who tried.
Always start in PortugueseDon't open with English
🏘️
Apartment building culture
Portuguese apartment culture is quiet and respectful. Keep noise down after 10pm, especially on weekdays. Greet neighbours in the lift or corridor. If you're having a gathering, warn neighbours in advance with a note. Shared stairwells and hallways are kept spotless — do your part.
Warn neighbours before a partyDon't play music loud after 10pm
🛒
Shopping at the market
At local markets, don't touch produce before buying — point and ask. Bargaining is not part of Portuguese culture except at flea markets. Bring a bag. Pay in cash where possible. Take your time and chat — the vendor relationship is part of the experience, not an obstacle to it.
Bring cash and a bagDon't squeeze the fruit
⚽
Football is religion
Portugal takes football seriously. Knowing that Benfica, Porto, and Sporting are fierce rivals — and which one your neighbour supports — is essential social knowledge. You don't have to like football, but respecting its importance in Portuguese culture opens more doors than almost anything else.
Learn your neighbour's teamDon't dismiss football as "just a game"
Honest guide
Things expats do that
quietly annoy locals
Written with kindness, not judgment. Most expats do these things without realising — awareness is the first step.
01
Complaining about Portugal publicly
Posting on social media or saying loudly in cafés that "things are so disorganised here" or "it's not like back home." Portuguese people are proud of their country — they can criticise it; guests shouldn't.
✓ Save comparisons for private conversations. Show gratitude for being welcomed.
02
Speaking loudly in English in local spaces
A neighbourhood tasca or local pharmacy isn't an international lounge. Dominating a space with loud English — and not attempting a word of Portuguese — signals that you don't really see the locals around you.
✓ Lower your volume. Smile. Try "bom dia" even if everything else is in English.
03
Only socialising with other expats
It's easy and comfortable to stick to the English-speaking bubble. But it means you're living in Portugal without really being in Portugal — and locals notice when entire neighbourhoods feel taken over by people who don't interact with them.
✓ One local friendship changes everything. Start with a language exchange.
04
Driving up rents without awareness
Many expats earn foreign salaries and can afford rents locals cannot. This is a structural problem, not personal fault — but being aware of it, and being a good tenant and community member, matters more than ever.
✓ Spend locally. Support independent Portuguese businesses over international chains.
05
Treating Portugal as a lifestyle accessory
"The weather is amazing, the food is great, the people are so friendly!" All true. But Portugal is a real country with real challenges — not a backdrop for a dream life. Locals can feel when they're being aestheticised rather than respected.
✓ Engage with Portugal's actual life — politics, language, community — not just its aesthetics.
06
Not learning any Portuguese after years of living here
This is the one locals mention most. After 2–3 years in Portugal, not knowing basic Portuguese signals that you don't feel the need to adapt — that Portugal should adapt to you. It's felt.
✓ 10 minutes a day on Practice Portuguese for 6 months changes this completely.

Volunteering in Portugal
The fastest way to make genuine Portuguese friends and feel part of the community. No Portuguese required for most opportunities.

Regular beach and coastal clean-up events across Portugal. A great way to contribute to the environment and meet both locals and expats who care about the same things.

English tutoring for Portuguese children
Lisbon, Porto, Algarve · Schools & community centres
Native English speakers are genuinely valuable to local schools and community centres. Even an hour a week helping children with conversational English is enormously appreciated and builds real community ties.
EducationHigh impact

Animal shelter volunteering
Every city · Canil municipal · Walking, socialising
Portugal's municipal animal shelters are always in need of volunteers to walk dogs and socialise cats. Contact your local câmara municipal or search "canil" + your city. No Portuguese needed — animals are universal.

Nationwide · Regular collection drives
Portugal's food banks run regular collection campaigns at supermarkets and need volunteers to help sort and distribute. The twice-yearly national drives are particularly well-organised and welcoming to new volunteers.

Cultural calendar
Portugal's year —
beyond the tourist highlights
The events, traditions, and rhythms that define Portuguese life. Participating — not just observing — is how you become part of it.
🎭
Carnaval (Feb)
Not as wild as Brazil but genuinely festive. Torres Vedras has the most celebrated Carnaval in Portugal — worth a trip from anywhere.
🌸
Semana Santa (Apr)
Holy Week processions across Portugal. Braga has the most atmospheric — candles, music, centuries of tradition. Deeply meaningful to locals.
🎵
Santos Populares (Jun)
The biggest party of the year. June saints' festivals fill Lisbon and Porto with sardines, sangria, music, and street dancing. Join in — this is Portugal at its most joyful.
🍇
Wine harvest (Sep)
The Douro Valley grape harvest is one of the most beautiful sights in Europe. Many quintas welcome volunteers. Absolutely worth experiencing.
🎃
Magusto (Nov)
St. Martin's Day — chestnuts roasted on every street corner, poncha and jeropiga to drink. A uniquely Portuguese autumn tradition most expats miss.
🐟
Bacalhau season
Salt cod (bacalhau) has 365 recipes — one for every day of the year. Ask a local their grandmother's recipe. It opens more conversations than almost anything.
⚽
Derby weekends
Benfica vs Porto or Sporting is a national event. You don't need to care about football — just know when it's happening and that everything stops.
🎶
Fado — year round
Skip the tourist fado houses. Ask a local where the real fado happens in your city. It's raw, emotional, and unlike anything else. Give it time — it grows on you.

Spend local
Support Portuguese businesses
Every euro spent at a locally-owned business stays in the community. Skip the chains — here are the kinds of places that make Portugal, Portugal.
🍽️
Find your local tasca
The neighbourhood tasca is the heartbeat of local Portugal. Handwritten menus, €8 lunch specials, the owner's mother in the kitchen. Walk until you find one that feels real. Then become a regular.
🛒
Shop at the mercado
Every city has a municipal market. Mercado da Ribeira, Bolhão, Loulé — the prices are often lower than supermarkets and the quality is higher. And you'll practice your Portuguese every visit.
☕
Café culture
A galão or café at a local café costs €0.80–1.20. Avoid the tourist café chains. Find the place where locals read the newspaper. Stand at the counter. Say "um café, por favor."
